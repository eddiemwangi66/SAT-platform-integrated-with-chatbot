Social engineering tactics

Social engineering attacks are a popular choice among threat actors. That’s because it’s often easier to trick people into providing them with access, information, or money than it is to exploit a software or network vulnerability.

As you might recall, social engineering is a manipulation technique that exploits human error to gain private information, access, or valuables. It's an umbrella term that can apply to a broad range of attacks. Each technique is designed to capitalize on the trusting nature of people and their willingness to help. In this reading, you will learn about specific social engineering tactics to watch out for. You’ll also learn ways that organizations counter these threats.

Types of Social Engineering Attacks:

1) Phishing: Attackers send fraudulent emails, messages, or websites to trick individuals into providing sensitive information, such as passwords or financial details.
2) Pretexting: Attackers create a fabricated scenario or pretext to trick individuals into revealing information or performing actions.
3) Baiting: Attackers offer something enticing, such as a free download or USB drive, containing malware or malicious links.
4) Spear Phishing: Targeted phishing attacks customized for specific individuals or organizations, often using personal information to increase credibility.
5) Vishing (Voice Phishing): Attackers use phone calls or voice messages to deceive individuals into providing sensitive information or performing actions.
6) Smishing (SMS Phishing): Phishing attacks conducted via text messages, typically containing malicious links or requests for sensitive information.
7) Tailgating: Physically following someone into a restricted area or building by exploiting their politeness or trust.
8) Impersonation: Pretending to be someone else, such as a trusted colleague or authority figure, to gain access to information or resources.

Recognizing Social Engineering Attempts

1) Requests for sensitive information, such as passwords, account numbers, or personal details, especially via unsolicited emails or messages.
2) Urgent or alarming messages urging immediate action, often with threats of consequences for non-compliance.
3) Unusual or unexpected requests, especially from unfamiliar or suspicious sources.
4) Requests for information that should not be shared publicly or with unauthorized individuals.
5) Unusual or uncharacteristic behavior from supposed colleagues or acquaintances.

Mitigating Social Engineering Risks:

1) Employee Training: Provide comprehensive training and awareness programs to educate employees about social engineering tactics and how to recognize and respond to them.
2) Security Policies and Procedures: Establish clear policies and procedures for handling sensitive information and verifying requests for information or actions.
3) Verification and Validation: Encourage individuals to verify requests for sensitive information or actions through alternative channels, such as contacting known phone numbers or email addresses.
4) Technological Controls: Implement email filtering, spam detection, and intrusion detection systems to identify and block social engineering attacks.
5) Incident Response: Develop incident response plans to quickly detect, respond to, and mitigate the impact of social engineering attacks.
